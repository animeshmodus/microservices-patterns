In one sentence: Define services around what the business does — its capabilities (Order Management, Delivery, Billing) — because capabilities are stable even as the how changes, so service boundaries drawn this way rarely need to move.

Use when

  • You're defining a service decomposition top-down from the business.
  • You want boundaries that map cleanly to team ownership.
  • The organisation already thinks in capabilities / value streams.

Be careful when

  • You're tempted to make a service per database table or technical layer.
  • Capabilities are defined by IT, not by business experts.
  • You haven't aligned team structure (Conway's Law will fight you).

The problem: where do the boundaries go?

You've decided to build microservices. But how many and which? Decompose badly and you get a distributed monolith or a swarm of chatty nano-services. You need a principled way to draw lines — and it must produce stable boundaries, because moving a boundary later (splitting a database, re-homing logic) is expensive.

The pattern: services mirror business capabilities

A business capability is something an organisation does to generate value — independent of how it's currently implemented. FTGO's capabilities include Order Management, Restaurant/Menu Management, Delivery Management, Accounting. You identify the capabilities (often hierarchically), then define one service per capability (or per leaf sub-capability).

Because capabilities reflect what the business is, not how it works today, they're stable. The implementation behind "Delivery Management" can change radically; the capability — and therefore the service boundary — stays put.

💡
Capability, not function or entity

Decompose around a capability (Order Management), not a verb (OrderController) or a table (an Address service). Capability boundaries are cohesive and coarse enough to avoid the chatty, brittle nano-services you get from one-service-per-entity.

How it works

  1. Identify capabilities from the business — analyse what the organisation does, its value streams, and the structure of the business. Workshops like Event Storming help.
  2. Decompose hierarchically if needed — e.g. Customer Management → Identity, Profile, Preferences — but only split when scaling or team needs justify it.
  3. Map each (sub)capability to a service that owns its data and exposes an API.
  4. Align teams to capabilities — apply the Inverse Conway Maneuver: shape teams to match the architecture you want.

Worked use cases

Use case · Banking

A retail bank's capability map

Top-level capabilities: Account Management, Lending, Payments, Fraud Detection, Customer Onboarding. Payments decomposes into Domestic Transfers, International Wire, Card Processing. A new "Buy Now Pay Later" product is then composed from Lending + Payments without touching Account Management — capability reuse in action.

Use case · E-commerce

Scaling only what's hot on Black Friday

Capabilities: Catalog, Inventory, Pricing & Promotions, Cart, Checkout, Fulfilment, Returns, Loyalty. The Black-Friday load hits Catalog and Pricing hardest — and because they're separate capability-services, they scale (scale-cube X-axis) independently of Fulfilment.

In the wild — tooling & practice

  • Team Topologies — its "stream-aligned team" maps directly onto a business capability owner; the most-cited modern framework for organising around services.
  • Event Storming (Alberto Brandolini) — a collaborative workshop to discover capabilities and their boundaries before any code.
  • Backstage (Spotify/CNCF) — an internal developer portal that catalogues which team owns which capability/service, essential once you have dozens.
  • Amazon & Spotify — capability-aligned ownership ("you build it, you run it"; squads aligned to Playlists, Discovery, Payments) are the textbook examples.

Pitfalls & anti-patterns

⚠
Watch out for these
  • Granularity mismatch — one service per CRUD entity instead of per capability → nano-services.
  • Shared database across "services" — kills independent deployability; it's a distributed monolith.
  • Ignoring Conway's Law — defining ideal services but keeping teams organised by tech layer; the org will reshape the architecture back.
  • Capabilities defined by IT rather than business experts — boundaries end up mirroring old silos.

Key takeaways

  • Draw service boundaries around business capabilities — stable because they describe what, not how.
  • Capability ≠ function ≠ entity; aim for cohesive, coarse-grained services.
  • Each capability-service owns its data and is owned by one team.
  • Pair with Decompose by subdomain — they usually agree and reinforce each other.
Check yourself

Why is "Order Management" a better service boundary than "OrderValidationService", "OrderPricingService" and "OrderPersistenceService"?

Show answer

The three technical services split one cohesive capability across the network: a single "place order" operation now needs multiple synchronous hops, shares order data awkwardly, and changes ripple across all three. "Order Management" keeps the cohesive logic and its data together, is independently deployable, and maps to one owning team.

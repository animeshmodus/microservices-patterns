In one sentence: A saga keeps data consistent across services by running a sequence of local transactions — one per service — where each step publishes a message that triggers the next, and any failure is undone by running compensating transactions in reverse.

Use when

  • A business operation spans two or more services (each with its own database).
  • You need consistency but cannot use a distributed (2PC/XA) transaction.
  • Eventual consistency is acceptable for the operation.

Avoid / be careful when

  • The operation must be strongly isolated (no intermediate states visible).
  • It fits inside one service / one database — just use a local ACID transaction.
  • Your team isn't ready for the debugging cost of asynchronous, multi-step flows.

The problem: one operation, many databases

In a microservice architecture, each service owns its own database — that's what makes services independently deployable. But many business operations naturally span several services. In the book's FTGO food-delivery app, Create Order must touch four services: it has to validate the Consumer, create a Ticket in the Kitchen, authorise the customer's card in Accounting, and create the Order itself.

In a monolith this is one local BEGIN … COMMIT transaction. Across services, the old answer was a distributed transaction using two-phase commit (2PC/XA). But 2PC is a poor fit for modern systems:

  • Many modern datastores (most NoSQL databases, and message brokers like Kafka) don't support it.
  • It's a synchronous, blocking protocol — if any participant is down, the whole operation is stuck, which reduces the availability of the entire system.
  • It couples services together at exactly the place microservices try to decouple them.
The saga pattern replaces one distributed ACID transaction with a series of small local transactions coordinated by messages. You trade isolation (the "I" in ACID) for availability and loose coupling — and then manage the consequences deliberately.

The pattern: local transactions stitched together

A saga is a sequence of steps. Each step is a local transaction in one service that updates its database and then publishes a message or event. That message triggers the next step. If step N fails, the saga executes compensating transactions that semantically undo steps N-1 … 1.

💡
Compensating transactions are not rollbacks

You can't ROLLBACK a transaction another service already committed. Instead you issue a new transaction that semantically reverses it — e.g. cancelTicket() after createTicket(), or refund() after charge(). Steps that can still abort cheaply are compensatable; the step that commits the point of no return is the pivot; everything after it is retriable.

How it works: two ways to coordinate

There are two flavours of saga, distinguished only by who decides the next step.

1. Choreography — the participants decide

There is no central coordinator. Each service subscribes to the others' domain events and reacts. It's simple and highly decoupled, but the overall flow is implicit — it lives in the wiring, not in one place.

2. Orchestration — a coordinator decides

A central saga orchestrator (often living inside the service that initiates the saga) tells each participant what to do via command messages and waits for reply messages. The flow is explicit and testable, and complex sagas stay manageable. This is the approach the book recommends for non-trivial sagas.

When a step fails, the orchestrator walks backwards, invoking each compensating command:

Worked use cases

Use case · FTGO food delivery (the book)

Create Order

The Create Order Saga orchestrator coordinates Consumer → Kitchen → Accounting. The pivot is authorising the card: before it, everything is compensatable (cancel ticket, reject order); after a successful authorisation, remaining steps are retriable (approve ticket, approve order). The order sits in a PENDING state — a semantic lock — until the saga completes.

Use case · Banking

Money transfer between two accounts at different services

Transfer ₹10,000 from a Checking service to a Savings service. Steps: (1) Checking debits ₹10,000 and records a TransferInitiated; (2) Savings credits ₹10,000. If the credit fails (account frozen), the compensation creditBack() reverses the debit. The account briefly shows a pending hold (semantic lock) so the customer can't double-spend the in-flight amount — a textbook commutative-update + pessimistic-view countermeasure combination.

Use case · Travel

Trip booking: flight + hotel + car

A booking saga reserves a flight, then a hotel, then a car. If the car step fails, it cancels the hotel and the flight. Because each provider is a separate system, a distributed transaction is impossible — a saga with clear compensations (and a time-boxed "reservation hold") is the standard solution.

The hard part: handling the lack of isolation

Because a saga is not isolated, other transactions can see its intermediate state, causing anomalies. The book names three and gives countermeasures you should design in from the start:

  • Lost updates — one saga overwrites another's change. Countermeasure: the semantic lock (a *_PENDING flag) so others know a saga is in progress.
  • Dirty reads — a transaction reads uncommitted saga state. Countermeasures: commutative updates (e.g. debit/credit commute) and reread value before acting.
  • Fuzzy / non-repeatable reads. Countermeasure: the by-value strategy — use the business risk of each request to choose between sagas and distributed transactions.

In the wild — tooling & who uses it

Sagas moved from "advanced pattern" to mainstream, largely because durable-execution and workflow engines now do the heavy lifting (state persistence, retries, timeouts, versioning):

  • Temporal (and its predecessor Cadence, born at Uber) — a durable-execution platform that treats a saga as long-running code (Go, Java, TypeScript, Python) and handles fault tolerance for you. The most popular code-first choice today.
  • Camunda 8 / Zeebe — BPMN diagrams as the source of truth; strong in regulated industries. Camunda publicly documents banks using its compensation events to implement sagas.
  • AWS Step Functions — a managed orchestrator; the "saga" reference pattern is in AWS's own guidance.
  • Eventuate Tram Sagas — Chris Richardson's own framework, the one used in the book's examples.
  • Axon Framework (JVM), MassTransit / NServiceBus (.NET) — saga support baked into messaging frameworks.

Real world: a bank's compensation saga

Garanti BBVA Teknoloji has written publicly about implementing orchestration-based sagas on Camunda 8, using BPMN compensation events so business analysts and engineers read the same diagram — a concrete example of why orchestration wins when auditability and complex branching matter.

Pitfalls & anti-patterns

⚠
Watch out for these
  • Forgetting compensations. Every compensatable step needs a tested "undo". Missing ones leave money or inventory stranded.
  • Non-idempotent handlers. Messages are delivered at least once; a participant that charges a card twice on a redelivery is a production incident. Make every step idempotent.
  • Assuming isolation. If you forget the countermeasures, you'll ship subtle "double-spend" bugs.
  • A god orchestrator that contains business logic belonging to participants — keep it to coordination.
  • Choreography for complex flows — beyond ~4 steps the implicit flow becomes impossible to reason about.

Key takeaways

  • A saga = a sequence of local transactions + compensating transactions, coordinated by messages.
  • You trade isolation for availability and decoupling — then add countermeasures (semantic locks, commutative updates).
  • Choreography (events, decentralised) for simple sagas; orchestration (a coordinator) for complex ones.
  • Reliable message publishing is mandatory — pair sagas with the transactional outbox.
  • Use a durable workflow engine (Temporal, Camunda 8) instead of hand-rolling state machines.
Check yourself

In the Create Order saga, why is authorising the credit card chosen as the pivot transaction rather than creating the order?

Show answer

Everything up to the card authorisation is cheaply reversible (cancel ticket, reject order), so those steps are compensatable. Authorising the card is the first step the customer truly commits to and that downstream steps depend on — after it succeeds the saga should only go forward (retriable). Designating it the pivot minimises the work that ever needs compensating and defines the point of no return.

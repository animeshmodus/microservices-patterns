Decomposition Strategies

How to slice a system into services along business capabilities and DDD subdomains.

Decompose by Business Capability

Decompose by business capability
In one sentence: Define services around what the business does — its capabilities (Order Management, Delivery, Billing) — because capabilities are stable even as the how changes, so service boundaries drawn this way rarely need to move.
Use when
  • You're defining a service decomposition top-down from the business.
  • You want boundaries that map cleanly to team ownership.
  • The organisation already thinks in capabilities / value streams.
Be careful when
  • You're tempted to make a service per database table or technical layer.
  • Capabilities are defined by IT, not by business experts.
  • You haven't aligned team structure (Conway's Law will fight you).

The problem: where do the boundaries go?

You've decided to build microservices. But how many and which? Decompose badly and you get a distributed monolith or a swarm of chatty nano-services. You need a principled way to draw lines — and it must produce stable boundaries, because moving a boundary later (splitting a database, re-homing logic) is expensive.

The pattern: services mirror business capabilities

A business capability is something an organisation does to generate value — independent of how it's currently implemented. FTGO's capabilities include Order Management, Restaurant/Menu Management, Delivery Management, Accounting. You identify the capabilities (often hierarchically), then define one service per capability (or per leaf sub-capability).

Because capabilities reflect what the business is, not how it works today, they're stable. The implementation behind "Delivery Management" can change radically; the capability — and therefore the service boundary — stays put.

💡
Capability, not function or entity

Decompose around a capability (Order Management), not a verb (OrderController) or a table (an Address service). Capability boundaries are cohesive and coarse enough to avoid the chatty, brittle nano-services you get from one-service-per-entity.

How it works

  1. Identify capabilities from the business — analyse what the organisation does, its value streams, and the structure of the business. Workshops like Event Storming help.
  2. Decompose hierarchically if needed — e.g. Customer Management → Identity, Profile, Preferences — but only split when scaling or team needs justify it.
  3. Map each (sub)capability to a service that owns its data and exposes an API.
  4. Align teams to capabilities — apply the Inverse Conway Maneuver: shape teams to match the architecture you want.

Worked use cases

Use case · Banking

A retail bank's capability map

Top-level capabilities: Account Management, Lending, Payments, Fraud Detection, Customer Onboarding. Payments decomposes into Domestic Transfers, International Wire, Card Processing. A new "Buy Now Pay Later" product is then composed from Lending + Payments without touching Account Management — capability reuse in action.

Use case · E-commerce

Scaling only what's hot on Black Friday

Capabilities: Catalog, Inventory, Pricing & Promotions, Cart, Checkout, Fulfilment, Returns, Loyalty. The Black-Friday load hits Catalog and Pricing hardest — and because they're separate capability-services, they scale (scale-cube X-axis) independently of Fulfilment.

In the wild — tooling & practice

  • Team Topologies — its "stream-aligned team" maps directly onto a business capability owner; the most-cited modern framework for organising around services.
  • Event Storming (Alberto Brandolini) — a collaborative workshop to discover capabilities and their boundaries before any code.
  • Backstage (Spotify/CNCF) — an internal developer portal that catalogues which team owns which capability/service, essential once you have dozens.
  • Amazon & Spotify — capability-aligned ownership ("you build it, you run it"; squads aligned to Playlists, Discovery, Payments) are the textbook examples.

Pitfalls & anti-patterns

⚠
Watch out for these
  • Granularity mismatch — one service per CRUD entity instead of per capability → nano-services.
  • Shared database across "services" — kills independent deployability; it's a distributed monolith.
  • Ignoring Conway's Law — defining ideal services but keeping teams organised by tech layer; the org will reshape the architecture back.
  • Capabilities defined by IT rather than business experts — boundaries end up mirroring old silos.

Key takeaways

  • Draw service boundaries around business capabilities — stable because they describe what, not how.
  • Capability ≠ function ≠ entity; aim for cohesive, coarse-grained services.
  • Each capability-service owns its data and is owned by one team.
  • Pair with Decompose by subdomain — they usually agree and reinforce each other.
Check yourself

Why is "Order Management" a better service boundary than "OrderValidationService", "OrderPricingService" and "OrderPersistenceService"?

Show answer

The three technical services split one cohesive capability across the network: a single "place order" operation now needs multiple synchronous hops, shares order data awkwardly, and changes ripple across all three. "Order Management" keeps the cohesive logic and its data together, is independently deployable, and maps to one owning team.

Decompose by Subdomain (DDD)

Decompose by subdomain
In one sentence: Use Domain-Driven Design to break the problem into subdomains, each with its own bounded context and consistent model — then make each bounded context a service. The boundary between two services is the place where the language of the business changes meaning.
Use when
  • The domain is rich and complex (the same word means different things to different teams).
  • You're already practising (or want) Domain-Driven Design.
  • You need to decide build vs buy per area (core vs generic).
Be careful when
  • You treat a single entity/table as a "bounded context".
  • You try to design the whole model up front instead of discovering it.
  • You let a shared kernel grow until contexts re-couple.

The problem: one "Order" means five different things

In FTGO, the word Order means something different to Order-taking, to the Kitchen, to Delivery and to Accounting. A single, shared "Order" class trying to satisfy everyone becomes a fuzzy, over-coupled mess. DDD gives us a vocabulary for turning these linguistic seams into service boundaries.

The pattern: subdomains & bounded contexts

Domain-Driven Design decomposes the domain (the whole problem space) into subdomains. Each subdomain has a bounded context — a boundary within which a single model and a single ubiquitous language are consistent. One bounded context = one service is the default.

Classify subdomains: core, supporting, generic

Not all subdomains deserve equal investment. DDD classifies them — and that drives build-vs-buy and where your best engineers go:

TypeWhat it isStrategyFTGO example
CoreYour competitive differentiatorInvest heavily, build in-house, best peopleDelivery scheduling / routing
SupportingNecessary but not differentiatingBuild simply, or outsourceRestaurant menu management
GenericA solved commodityBuy / use OSS, wrap with an ACLPayments (Stripe), identity, email

How it works: context mapping

Once you have bounded contexts, you classify the relationships between them (a context map). The relationship dictates the integration style and which team depends on which.

Relationships include Partnership, Customer–Supplier, Conformist, Shared Kernel, Open Host Service / Published Language, and the Anti-Corruption Layer (used whenever you integrate a legacy or generic system without letting its model leak in).

Worked use cases

Use case · Banking

Loan origination — same word, two models

Core: Credit Decisioning (proprietary risk models) with a rich domain model (CreditApplication aggregate, RiskScore value object). Generic: Identity Verification — a thin ACL over a vendor (e.g. Jumio). This classification tells you to staff Credit Decisioning with senior engineers and merely integrate identity.

Use case · Ride-hailing

"Driver" is two different things

In the Matching context a Driver is a lightweight struct (location + availability). In the Driver Management context a Driver is a rich aggregate (profile, documents, earnings, compliance). Same word, two models in two bounded contexts, connected via a Customer–Supplier relationship with an ACL — exactly the seam DDD predicts.

In the wild — tooling & practice

  • Event Storming & Domain Storytelling — workshops to discover subdomains, events and aggregates collaboratively.
  • Context Mapper — an open-source DSL for modelling bounded contexts and their relationships (generates diagrams & service stubs).
  • Microsoft eShopOnContainers — a canonical reference app where each service maps to a DDD bounded context (Catalog, Ordering, Basket, Identity, Payment).
  • Spring Modulith (2023+) — enforces bounded-context boundaries inside a modular monolith and can generate context maps from code; a great stepping stone before extracting services.

Pitfalls & anti-patterns

⚠
Watch out for these
  • Bounded context = one table. A real context encapsulates a business process, not a data structure.
  • Anemic model. Using DDD vocabulary but writing transaction-script logic — overhead without benefit.
  • Shared-kernel sprawl. Too many shared types re-couple the contexts you split.
  • Big design up front. Discover the model iteratively from working code + events, don't pre-design it.

Key takeaways

  • Decompose the domain into subdomains; each bounded context → one service.
  • The service boundary is where the ubiquitous language changes meaning.
  • Classify subdomains core / supporting / generic to direct investment and build-vs-buy.
  • Make context relationships explicit; protect contexts with an anti-corruption layer.
  • This usually agrees with decompose by business capability — use both.
Check yourself

Your "Customer" means different things to Sales (a lead with a pipeline stage) and to Support (an account with tickets). What does DDD say to do?

Show answer

That linguistic divergence is the bounded-context boundary. Don't build one shared Customer model — create two contexts (Sales and Support), each with its own Customer model and language, and integrate them via events or an API (with an ACL if one must conform to the other). Forcing a single model is what creates the fuzzy, over-coupled mess.

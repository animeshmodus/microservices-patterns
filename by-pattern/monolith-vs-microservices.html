Monolith vs. Microservice Architecture

The monolithic and microservice architectural styles, the scale cube, and when each one wins.

Monolithic architectureMicroservice architecture
In one sentence: The monolithic style packages all of an application's functionality into a single deployable unit; the microservice style structures it as a set of small, independently deployable services organised around business capabilities — and the whole book is about doing the second one without the pain.

Reach for microservices when

  • A large team is blocked by a single codebase & release train.
  • Different parts need to scale or evolve independently.
  • You need fault isolation and polyglot tech choices.

Stay monolithic (for now) when

  • The product/domain is still being discovered (boundaries unclear).
  • The team is small and the app is not yet large.
  • You lack the operational maturity (CI/CD, containers, observability).

The problem: "monolithic hell"

The book opens with FTGO, a successful food-delivery company whose single, large application has slowed to a crawl. The monolith was great at first — easy to develop, test, deploy and scale (just run more copies). But as the codebase and team grew, it descended into what Richardson calls monolithic hell:

  • Slow delivery. A small change requires understanding, testing and redeploying the entire application; releases are big, risky and infrequent.
  • An intimidating, brittle codebase that no one fully understands.
  • Scaling is all-or-nothing — you can't scale just the part that's hot.
  • Locked into an ageing tech stack; trying a new language/framework means rewriting everything.

Two architectural styles

The defining trait of the microservice architecture isn't size — it's that each service is independently deployable and owns its own database. That single rule is what enables team autonomy, and it's also the source of every hard problem in this course (queries and transactions that now span services).

The scale cube — there's more than one way to scale

From The Art of Scalability, the scale cube models three independent scaling axes. Microservices are just one of them (the Y-axis); many systems only ever need the X-axis.

Benefits & drawbacks

Benefits

  • Continuous delivery of large, complex apps; small, frequent, low-risk releases.
  • Services are small, understandable and independently scalable.
  • Fault isolation and freedom to adopt new tech per service.
  • Team autonomy — "two-pizza teams" own services end to end.

Drawbacks

  • Finding the right service boundaries is genuinely hard.
  • Distributed systems are complex: partial failure, network, async.
  • Operations & testing require real maturity (CI/CD, observability).
  • Cross-service queries & transactions need composition/sagas.

Not a silver bullet: the "microservice premium"

Microservices buy you team-scaling, but at a real operational cost — Martin Fowler calls it the microservice premium. If your app isn't big enough (or your team mature enough) to pay it, a well-structured modular monolith is often the better call.

🎥
Real world: when microservices were the wrong answer

In 2023 Amazon Prime Video published a now-famous post describing how they moved an audio/video-monitoring pipeline from a distributed microservices + Step Functions design back to a single process — cutting infrastructure cost ~90%. The lesson isn't "monoliths win"; it's that architecture is a per-use-case trade-off. Shopify deliberately runs a modular monolith (Rails, with the packwerk tool enforcing module boundaries) at enormous scale.

In the wild

  • Amazon — migrated off a monolith from ~2002; the Bezos "API mandate" + two-pizza teams are the canonical origin story (and ultimately birthed AWS).
  • Netflix — hundreds of services on AWS, backed by heavy investment in resilience tooling (Chaos Monkey, Eureka, Zuul, later Resilience4j).
  • Uber — grew to ~2,000+ services, then introduced Domain-Oriented Microservice Architecture (DOMA) to tame the sprawl by grouping services into domains.
  • Shopify / Stack Overflow — proof that disciplined monoliths scale beautifully too.

Pitfalls & anti-patterns

⚠
Watch out for these
  • The distributed monolith — separately deployed services that are still tightly coupled (shared DB, synchronous call chains). Worst of both worlds.
  • Nano-services — splitting so finely that a single operation needs a dozen network hops.
  • Premature decomposition — carving boundaries before the domain is understood, then paying to move them.
  • "Mono bad, micro good" reflex — choosing the style for fashion, not for a measured trade-off.

Key takeaways

  • The microservice architecture is a tool for fast delivery by large teams — not a goal in itself.
  • Its defining property: independently deployable services, each owning its data.
  • It carries a real premium; small/young systems are usually better as modular monoliths.
  • Use the scale cube to check whether you actually need Y-axis decomposition.
  • If you do decompose, do it incrementally with the Strangler pattern.
Check yourself

Two teams ship a "microservices" system but can never deploy one service without the other. What anti-pattern is this, and what's the most likely root cause?

Show answer

It's a distributed monolith. The most common root cause is a shared database (or synchronous request chains that assume the other service is always up). The fix is data ownership per service plus asynchronous, well-contracted integration — restoring true independent deployability.
